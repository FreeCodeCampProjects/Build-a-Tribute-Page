carl sagan

scientist, author, and educator


intro

Carl Sagan (1934–1996) was an American astronomer, science communicator, author, media personality, and advocate for skepticism and critical thinking. During an illustrious academic career, he contributed in excess of 600 papers on topics related to astronomy, cosmology, astrophysics and astrobiology. He was Miller Fellow at the University of California, Berkeley, a lecturer at Cornell University and a trustee of the SETI Institute, who named their Mountain View facility after him: the Carl Sagan Center.

Beyond academia, Sagan is credited as author, co-author, or editor on over 20 book titles—the sci-fi novel "Contact" (later adapted into a film featuring Jodie Foster) his magnum opus. On television, he narrated and co-wrote the award-winning series "Cosmos: A Personal Journey", which—as of 2009—was still the most frequently watched PBS series in the world.

honours

Sagan won many awards throughout his life. The following are among his most notable:

  • Miller Institute's "Miller Research Fellowship" (1960–1962)
  • Klumpke-Roberts' "Award of the Astronomical Society of the Pacific" (1974)
  • National Aeronautics and Space Administration's "NASA Distinguished Public Service Medal" (1977)
  • The Ohio State University's "Annual Award for Television Excellence" (for PBS series 'Cosmos') (1981)
  • Hugo Award for 'Cosmos' (1981)
  • American Astronautical Society's "John F. Kennedy Astronautics Award" (1982)
  • Committee for the Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal's "Isaac Asimov Award" (1994)
  • SF Chronicle Award (for 'Contact') (1998)
